What does Diotima actually teach Socrates about love?

AISynthesis · Qwen3-1.7B · grounded in 4 sources
hybrid retrieval · k=5 · 318ms

Diotima begins by overturning the picture of Eros as a god, telling Socrates that love is instead a great spirit, an intermediary between the mortal and the divine. She frames love as a kind of in-betweenness — born of Poverty and Resource, never sated, never destitute, always in pursuit of what it lacks.

The ladder of beauty

Her central teaching is the famous ascent: the lover begins with the beauty of one body, then sees the beauty common to all bodies, then the beauty of souls, of laws, and finally of knowledge itself. At the top of the ladder waits Beauty itself, “eternal and unchanging,” an object the philosopher comes to love above any single instance of it.

For Diotima, love is finally the longing for immortality through creation — physical in the body, spiritual in the production of wisdom and virtue.

And how is that different from what Pausanias says about love earlier in the dialogue?

AISynthesis · Qwen3-1.7B · grounded in 3 sources
hybrid retrieval · k=5 · 287ms

Pausanias divides love along the lines of its patron goddesses: “there are two goddesses, and therefore two loves — the elder, who has no mother, the daughter of Uranus, whom we call heavenly; and the younger, daughter of Zeus and Dione, whom we call common.” The “common” Aphrodite governs an indiscriminate, bodily love, while the “heavenly” one moves toward the soul.

Diotima keeps the moral hierarchy but discards the dual-goddess scaffolding entirely. Love is one thing — a single restless spirit — and what differs is the object the lover is climbing toward at any given rung of the ascent. Where Pausanias sorts lovers, Diotima sorts loves; the same person can begin in the bodily and climb to the contemplative.

Quote the line where Alcibiades compares Socrates to a Silenus statue.

AISynthesis · Qwen3-1.7B · grounded in 1 source
hybrid retrieval · k=3 · 204ms

Alcibiades introduces the comparison just after bursting into the party drunk and being asked to give his own praise of love — he turns the speech on Socrates instead:

“I say that he is exactly like the busts of Silenus, which are set up in the statuaries' shops, holding pipes and flutes in their mouths; and they are made to open in the middle.”

The figure is meant to be both joke and confession — the outer Silenus is ugly and grotesque, but split it open and you find “images of the gods” inside.
